In other words, all we really need to understand is differentiation. Integrals are just an application of differentiation! So long as we can get good at moving *backwards* through differentiation to *pre* or *anti* derivatives, we can get the value of integrals exactly and quickly. For example, letâ€™s look at \(x^2-1\) from the earlier section. A little thought reveals that
\begin{equation*} \left( \frac{x^3}{3} - x \right)' = x^2 - 1 \end{equation*}
So, \(\frac{x^3}{3} - x\) is an antiderivative of \(x^2 - 1\) and according to the FTC, we have
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{lll} \int_{-1}^2 (x^2-1)dx \amp = \amp \left(\frac{2^3}{3}-2 \right) - \left(\frac{(-1)^3}{3}-(-1) \right) \\ \amp = \amp \left(\frac{8}{3}-2 \right) - \left(\frac{-1}{3} + 1 \right)\\ \amp = \amp \frac{8}{3}-2 + \frac{1}{3}-1 \\ \amp = \amp \frac{9}{3}-3\\ \amp = \amp 3-3 \\ \amp = \amp 0 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
Just as we found with the limit of Riemann sums earlier!
