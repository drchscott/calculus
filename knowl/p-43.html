So... how? How did historical mathematical luminaries such as Isaac Newton solve this tangent line problem? Well, suppose for a moment that instead of looking at the tangent line, we look at a line passing through \((1,1)\) and a point on the graph just a teensy bit to the right or left of \((1,1)\text{.}\) So, suppose \(h\) is a teensy number. We could adjust \(1\) by that amount and look up at the graph to the point \((1+h, f(1+h))\text{.}\) For example, we could take \(h=.75\) giving us the point on the graph \((1.75,1.75^2)=(1.75,3.0625)\text{.}\) The line going through the two points \((1,1)\) and \((1.75,1.75^2)=(1.75,3.0625)\) has a slope (rise/run) equal to \((3.0625-1)/(1.75-1) = 2.75\) (i.e. \((f(1+h)-f(1))/h\)). So, that line *can* be plotted since we have both a slope, \(2.75\)), and a point the line passes through, \((1,1)\text{.}\) Here, let’s plot that line as a dashed line and show it along with the tangent line.
You’ll notice that while this dashed line is not quite the tangent line, it is ... close. We could get even closer by choosing a point even closer to \((1,1)\text{.}\) In other words, make \(h\) smaller (either a teensier positive or negative number). For smaller and smaller values of \(h\text{,}\) the resulting lines should look more and more line the tangent line.
